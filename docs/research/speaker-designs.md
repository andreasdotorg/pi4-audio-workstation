### 3.2 Scott Hinson's DIYRM MEH

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Driver complement** | Multiple drivers in a shared horn — typically 1x 15" woofer + 2x mid drivers + 1x compression driver (or similar), all loaded by the same horn mouth |
| **Crossover topology** | MEH (Multiple Entry Horn) — 3-way or 4-way with all drivers sharing a common horn |
| **Enclosure type** | Horn-loaded (shared horn with multiple entry points at different depths) |
| **Crossover frequency** | Typically ~200-400 Hz (bass to mid), ~1.5-3 kHz (mid to HF) |
| **Power handling** | Varies by driver selection |
| **Impedance** | Varies by wiring configuration |
| **Special characteristics** | MEH (Multiple Entry Horn) is a design philosophy where multiple drivers of different frequency ranges share a single horn. Each driver enters the horn at a different point along its length — the HF driver at the throat, mid driver partway down, woofer at/near the mouth. This creates a true point-source with coherent phase alignment. Scott Hinson is a well-known contributor on diyAudio forums. "DIYRM" = DIY Room/Reference Monitor. MEH designs require careful horn geometry and driver placement to achieve proper acoustic summation. The horn path length for each driver determines the acoustic offset — critical for phase-coherent crossover design. |

### 4.2 15" Onken Subs

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Driver complement** | 1x or 2x 15" woofers |
| **Crossover topology** | Subwoofer / bass cabinet |
| **Enclosure type** | Onken = a specific bass reflex variant with a wide, shallow port (slot port) across the full width of the baffle. Originally designed by a Japanese audiophile in the 1950s-60s for hi-fi use, adapted for PA. |
| **Crossover frequency** | Typically below 150-200 Hz |
| **Power handling** | Varies by driver — 400-800W per 15" driver |
| **Impedance** | 8 Ω (single), 4 Ω (dual parallel) |
| **Special characteristics** | The Onken enclosure uses a wide slot port that provides even air flow and reduced port compression at high levels. The large port area reduces turbulence noise. Popular in Japanese and European audiophile communities, and adapted for PA use where clean bass reproduction is valued. Less common in modern PA than horn-loaded or reflex subs, but valued for musical quality. 15" drivers offer a balance of extension and punch — faster transient response than 18" at the cost of less deep extension and displacement. |

### 4.3 15" Horn Subs

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Driver complement** | 1x or 2x 15" woofers |
| **Crossover topology** | Subwoofer |
| **Enclosure type** | Front-loaded horn or folded horn (tapped horn, scoop, W-bin, or similar) |
| **Crossover frequency** | Below 100-150 Hz (horn-loaded subs have a natural rolloff above the horn's passband) |
| **Power handling** | 400-1000W per driver |
| **Impedance** | Varies |
| **Special characteristics** | Horn-loaded subs use a folded or straight horn to increase acoustic efficiency in the low-frequency passband. The horn provides +6-12 dB of acoustic gain within its passband but limits bandwidth — horn subs typically have a narrower usable range than direct-radiating reflex subs. Popular variants: scoop (front-loaded folded horn), tapped horn (uses both front and rear radiation), W-bin (double-folded). 15" horn subs are more common in older/traditional designs; modern PA tends toward 18" or 21" for sub-bass. The 15" horn sub excels at mid-bass punch (60-120 Hz) rather than deep sub-bass. |
